Present

1 / Foundations
State and Dynamic Verbs

State and dynamic verbs

State verbs

A state verb describes a condition or an existence.

be, hate, like
↔

Dynamic verbs

A dynamic verb describes an action or event.

do, play, jump

Key idea

State verbs normally describe what someone is, has, feels, or thinks.

Key idea

Dynamic verbs normally describe what someone does or what is happening.

Why it matters

This distinction helps explain why some verbs are rarely used in the progressive form.

3 / Present Time
Simple vs Progressive

Present simple and present progressive

Present Simple

Use it for habits, states, truths, and programmes

I take the bus to work every day. - habitual action
He lives in England. - state
Water boils at 100 degrees Centigrade. - eternal truth
Lessons start at 9:00 a.m. - timetable
Present Progressive

Use it for temporary, unfinished, or repeated limited action

I’m washing my face at the moment. - temporary short action
I’m reading “Wuthering Heights.” - unfinished action
I’m visiting a gym nowadays. - repeated temporary action
He’s always telling me what to do. - annoying habit
Whenever I visit him, he’s working. - repeated action within limits
4 / Present Time
Perfect Forms

Present perfect simple and progressive

Present Perfect Simple

Past action or state with a present connection

I’ve known them for ages. - state lasting until now
I’ve seen that film. - indefinite event in the past
She’s called ten times this week. - repeated action until now
He’s broken his arm. - the result is still obvious
Present Perfect Progressive

Duration or visible recent activity

It’s been raining hard all day. - activity that started in the past and is still happening
Have you been working in the garden? - activity that only stopped a while ago but has an obvious result
How long have you been seeing this doctor? - repeated activity at certain times

Simple perfect focuses on...

the fact, the experience, or the result.

Perfect progressive focuses on...

the activity, the process, or the duration.

5 / Present Time
Simple or Progressive?

Verbs that are hardly ever used in the progressive

The verb be

Usually not progressive in its normal state meaning.

Emotion

like, dislike, wish, desire, care

Thinking

feel (= believe), know, realise, believe, suppose, mean, remember, mind, see (= understand)

Possession

own, belong, have (= possess)

Senses

see, hear, sound, smell, notice, recognise

Other common verbs

seem, appear, look (= seem), cost, want

To emphasise that the action is temporary, we can sometimes use can with these verbs:
I can see you now.
6 / Present Time
Meaning Changes

Some verbs can be progressive with a change of meaning

State meaning

Eric is stupid.
All the time.

Progressive meaning

Eric is being stupid about the whole situation.
At the moment.

State meaning

What do you think about the new student?
What is your opinion?

Progressive meaning

What are you thinking about?
What is going on in your mind now?

State meaning

This soup tastes great.
A characteristic of the soup.

Progressive meaning

He’s tasting the soup to see if it needs more salt.
Actually putting soup in his mouth.

State meaning

The flowers smell nice.
A characteristic of the flowers.

Progressive meaning

The dog is smelling him.
Actually doing it.

State meaning

I don’t see what you mean.
Understand.

Progressive meaning

I’m seeing my doctor next week.
Meeting.

Watch out

Remember to check the spelling rules and keep the meaning in focus.

Also remember

He’s been to Paris = he visited it before.
He’s gone to Paris = he is there now.

Passive note: the Present Perfect Progressive is not used in the passive voice.

8 / Past Time
Main Contrasts

Past time: the main contrasts

Past Simple
I usually worked from 9 to 5. - past habit
All of a sudden she walked out of the room. - telling a story
Past Progressive
While he was typing his report, it started raining heavily. - longer action with a sudden event
While I was doing my homework, my sister was fixing her bike. - two simultaneous actions
He was always complaining about money. - annoying habit
Used to / Would
I used to walk to work when I lived downtown. - past habit
She used to like to stay out till late. - past habitual state
After dinner we would open our presents... - repeated past event in reminiscence
Past Perfect Simple
When I got to the station the train had already left. - past before past
Past Perfect Progressive
He felt sick because he had been drinking all night. - past before past with emphasis on duration
9 / Past Time
Watch Outs

Past time watch outs

Past Progressive

It is not used to describe habitual events or states in the past. Use used to for that purpose.

Used to

Used to exists only in the past and can be replaced by the simple past.

Would

Would is used for habitual events, especially when we reminisce. It is not used for habitual states.

Passive note

The Past Perfect Progressive is not used in the passive.

Possible

I used to ride a bicycle.
I would ride a bicycle, whenever I had the chance.

Not possible

She used to be a famous opera singer.
She would be a famous opera singer. - state

Contrast with the present

I used to / would play football, but now I don’t.

Reason

The present contrast now I don’t makes would impossible there.

11 / Future Time
Main Forms

Future time forms

“will” Future
I’ll probably pass the exam. - prediction
That’ll be John at the door. - assumption
I’ll buy this one. - immediate decision
I’ll give you the money you need. - promise
I’ll do it for you if you like. - offer of help
“going to” Future
I’m going to visit my grandmother. - intention / plan already made
Clouds are gathering. It’s going to rain. - prediction based on present evidence
Present Progressive as Future
I’m visiting the doctor tomorrow. - fixed arrangement
Future Progressive
I’ll be working on my essay. - action in progress at a future point
I’ll be needing it for my work next week. - action that will happen anyway
This time next year she will be studying in England. - fixed arrangement or plan
Future Perfect
By next month, I will have finished the book. - completed by a future time
At the end of summer, I will have been working with them for three years. - emphasis on process or duration
12 / Future Time
Watch Outs

Future time watch outs

No future form after time words or if

When we want to show future time, we use the Present or the Present Perfect, not will.

When I have finished my work, I’ll come.
If I see him tomorrow, I’ll tell him.

Future in the past

We often use was / were going to for something intended in the past that did not happen.

I was going to visit my aunt yesterday but something happened and I didn’t.

“Going to” vs Present Progressive

I’m visiting Sue tomorrow. - the speaker simply states the future action
I’m going to visit Sue tomorrow. - the speaker shows the wish or intention for it to happen

“Will” or “Going to”?

We have run out of milk. I know. I’m going to get some. - decision made before speaking
We have run out of milk. Really? I’ll get some. - decision made at the moment of speaking
13 / Practice
Scenario set 1

Scenario practice 1

Choose the best tense, say the full sentence aloud, then flip the card to check.

1. Monday routine

Every Monday, there ar piano lessons for Ella after school.

Use: go
Best fit: Present Simple.
Ella goes to piano lessons every Monday. - habit / routine
2. Right now

I can't come out now because I am in the middle of my homework.

Use: do
Best fit: Present Progressive.
I’m doing my homework now. - action in progress at the moment
3. Life experience

Maria knows Madrid well because she lived there for three years and that experience matters now.

Use: live
Best fit: Present Perfect Simple.
Maria has lived in Madrid for three years. - duration up to now / present relevance
4. Background action

When we arrived home, Dad was in the kitchen in the middle of making dinner.

Use: cook
Best fit: Past Progressive.
Dad was cooking when we arrived. - background action in progress in the past
5. Visible evidence

Look at those black clouds. The prediction comes from what we can see now.

Use: rain
Best fit: Going to Future.
It’s going to rain. - prediction based on present evidence
6. By next June

The bridge project will be complete before next June arrives.

Use: finish
Best fit: Future Perfect.
They will have finished the bridge by next June. - completion by a future time
14 / Practice
Scenario set 2

Scenario practice 2

Focus on meaning first: duration, arrangement, earlier past, state verb, habit, or future time clause.

1. Cause before past result

He felt exhausted because the drinking started earlier and continued for hours before that moment in the past.

Use: drink
Best fit: Past Perfect Progressive.
He felt sick because he had been drinking all night. - earlier past cause with emphasis on duration
2. Fixed arrangement

We already have an appointment tomorrow at ten.

Use: see
Best fit: Present Progressive for future arrangement.
We’re seeing the doctor tomorrow at ten. - arranged future
3. Repeated experience

This is the third time I watch this film.

Use: see
Best fit: Present Perfect Simple.
I’ve seen this film three times. - repeated experience up to now
4. Past habit

Grandma had a regular old habit: she sat by the window and read there every afternoon.

Use: sit / read
Best fit: Used to.
Grandma used to sit by the window and read every afternoon. - past habit / repeated past action
5. State

You already have the answer in your mind.

Use: know
Best fit: Present Simple with a state verb.
I know the answer. - state, not progressive
6. Future time clause

First I will finish the work. After that, I will call you.

Use: finish / call - Remember: after when, English does not use will.
Best fit: Present Perfect after a time word.
When I have finished my work, I’ll call you. - future meaning after a time word without will
15 / Recap
Whole System

The tense system in one detailed view

Present Time

general truthaction nowresult / duration now

Present Simple - habits, routines, states, truths, timetables

Present Progressive - temporary action, action now, annoying habit

Present Perfect Simple - experience, result, repeated action up to now

Present Perfect Progressive - duration, process, recent visible activity

Past Time

habit / storybackground actionpast before past

Past Simple - finished event, story step, past habit

Past Progressive - background scene, longer action in progress, parallel action

Used to / Would - repeated past behaviour; would not for states

Past Perfect / Past Perfect Progressive - earlier past cause, result, or duration before another past point

Future Time

prediction / decisionplan / arrangementcompletion by future time

Will - prediction, promise, offer, instant decision

Going to - prior intention, evidence-based prediction

Present Progressive - fixed arrangement in the future

Future Progressive / Future Perfect - action in progress later, or completed by a future time

Quick decision guide

Ask first: What time is this about? Then ask: What meaning do I want to show? Choose between habit, action in progress, result, duration, background action, earlier past, intention, arrangement, or completion.

Final watch outs

State verbs are rarely progressive, would does not describe habitual states, and after when / if / before / after / until English normally uses a present form, not will.
